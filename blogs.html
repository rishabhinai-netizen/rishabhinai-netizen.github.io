01
Mortgage Fraud · BFSI June 2026
The Anatomy of Mortgage Fraud in Indian Housing Finance
India's housing finance sector has grown at an extraordinary pace — and fraud has grown alongside it. Unlike corporate fraud, mortgage fraud in India rarely involves a single bad actor. It is almost always a network: a borrower, a DMA, sometimes a valuer, and occasionally an insider. Understanding the anatomy of this network is the first step to dismantling it.
🏠
The Document Layer — Where Fraud Begins
Most mortgage fraud originates in the document stack — forged salary slips, manipulated bank statements, and fabricated ITRs. The sophisticated actor doesn't create obviously fake documents; they create plausible ones that pass a cursory review. The tell is usually in the metadata: font inconsistencies, round-number salary figures, and bank statement balances that don't reflect realistic savings patterns relative to income.
🤝
The DMA Problem — Your Distribution Network as Attack Surface
Direct Marketing Associates are the primary origination channel for most HFCs — and the primary fraud entry point. A rogue DMA has incentives aligned exactly opposite to the lender: they earn on disbursement, not repayment. Jaro-Winkler name deduplication across DMA registrations, geographic clustering of fraud-linked accounts, and conversion rate outlier analysis are the three most effective detective controls I've found.
💸
The Fund Flow Pattern — The Forensic Signature
Every mortgage fraud that involves misappropriation of loan proceeds leaves a fund flow signature. Structuring below STR thresholds, rapid in-and-out movement within 24–72 hours of disbursement, and circular transfers through related accounts are the three most common patterns. Cross-bank self-transfer detection — especially across accounts the borrower hasn't disclosed — is where forensic bank statement analysis yields the clearest evidence.
🔗
The CERSAI Gap — India's Underused Fraud Deterrent
The Central Registry of Securitisation Asset Reconstruction and Security Interest exists precisely to prevent double financing on the same property. Yet its systematic integration into pre-disbursement due diligence remains inconsistent across lenders. A CERSAI check that flags an existing encumbrance should be a hard stop — not a soft exception that gets waived for business reasons.
The bottom line: Mortgage fraud in India is a systems problem, not a people problem. No single control prevents it — what works is layered detection: document forensics, DMA screening, CERSAI verification, and post-disbursement fund flow monitoring operating simultaneously. The HFCs that will win on fraud prevention are the ones that build detection into origination, not investigation into default.
02
AML · AI · PMLA May 2026
How AI Is Changing Suspicious Transaction Reporting in India
The FIU-IND received over 11 lakh Suspicious Transaction Reports in FY2024. A significant portion were filed to cover regulatory exposure, not because they were genuinely suspicious. AI doesn't fix that cultural problem — but it can fundamentally change the quality and speed of the reports that matter.
📋
The STR Drafting Problem
A well-drafted STR requires the investigator to construct a coherent narrative — the who, what, how, and why of suspicious behaviour — often under time pressure and with fragmented data across multiple systems. Most investigators are excellent at investigation but not at structured narrative writing. An AI model given a clear fund flow summary, the PMLA category codes, and the relevant account history can produce a first draft in seconds that the investigator then refines. That's not replacing judgment — it's removing the blank-page problem.
🔍
Pattern Detection at Scale
The most sophisticated AML evasion techniques exploit the gap between what a human can review and what the transaction volume demands. A structuring scheme running ₹2–3 lakh per transaction across 40 accounts over 90 days is invisible to manual review but trivial to detect with the right query. The challenge isn't the detection algorithm — it's the data quality and integration that feeds it.
🤖
What AI Does Well — and What It Doesn't
AI is excellent at pattern surface, narrative construction, classification assistance, and regulatory cross-referencing. It is poor at exercising judgment about context — whether an unusual transaction is suspicious or simply reflects a life event the algorithm doesn't understand. The investigator's judgment remains irreplaceable; AI is the force multiplier that handles the volume so the investigator can focus on the cases that actually matter.
🏦
The Indian Context — HFCs and the PMLA Gap
Housing Finance Companies have historically had less mature AML infrastructure than scheduled commercial banks. The NHB's increasing regulatory focus and the PMLA 2002 obligations that apply equally to HFCs mean that the compliance gap is narrowing fast — but the tooling gap persists. Most HFCs are still running STR workflows on spreadsheets and institutional knowledge. That's the gap I've been building tools to close.
The bottom line: AI in AML for Indian financial institutions is not a future state — it's a present opportunity that most institutions are leaving on the table. The regulatory expectation is moving faster than the technology adoption curve. The HFCs and NBFCs that invest in structured AI-assisted compliance workflows now will be better positioned for the next FATF Mutual Evaluation than those waiting for a packaged solution.

AI Tools · BFSI · No-Code April 2026
Building No-Code Forensic Tools for BFSI Investigation Teams
The average fraud investigation team in an Indian BFSI institution has access to Excel, a core banking system login, and a shared folder. They are asked to investigate increasingly complex cases — multi-lender fraud networks, sophisticated bank statement manipulation, shell entity structures — with tools that haven't changed in a decade. The gap between what investigators need and what technology teams can deliver (at the pace investigators need it) is where no-code and low-code tooling becomes genuinely transformative.
🛠️
The Single-File HTML Principle
Every internal forensic tool I've built is a single HTML file that runs entirely in the browser — no server, no installation, no IT approval process. An investigator downloads the file, opens it in Chrome, and has a working tool. This sounds like a constraint but is actually the right architecture for the BFSI context: investigation teams operate in restricted environments where deploying cloud tools requires months of security review. A self-contained HTML file bypasses that entirely while keeping all data local.
🔬
What StaffLens Taught Me About Forensic UX
When I built StaffLens — the bank statement forensic analysis tool — the first version was technically correct but unusable. Investigators had to understand the detection logic to interpret the output. The second version restructured the output around investigator workflow: flagged transactions first, with plain-English explanations of why each was flagged. The lesson: forensic tools built for experts still need to present findings as if to a generalist. The expert then validates the machine's work, not the other way around.
⚡
The Claude API as Investigation Analyst
The AML Knowledge Hub experiment — using Claude via Groq API as a conversational compliance assistant — revealed something important: the most useful AI interaction for investigators is not "summarise this document" but "here is this fund flow pattern, what PMLA category does it map to, and what additional evidence should I be looking for?" That specificity of query design is what separates useful AI integration from a glorified search engine.
📈
The Dual Stack Advantage
Having built both fraud investigation tools and equity analytics platforms, I've found that the mental model transfers in unexpected ways. The pattern detection logic in StaffLens (cross-bank self-transfers, round-trip funds) uses the same algorithmic thinking as the structuring detection in CARA (rapid in-and-out fund cycling in F&O positions). Financial crime and market microstructure share more DNA than either field typically acknowledges.
The bottom line: The best forensic tools for BFSI teams aren't the most sophisticated — they're the ones that investigators actually use. Single-file offline deployment, workflow-first UX, and AI assistance focused on specific investigative queries are the three design principles that have made the difference in everything I've shipped. The technology isn't the barrier; the design thinking is.
